Visualizing The Naqab
Visualizing the Naqab is a platform that aims to make evident existing systems of governance manifested as abuse of territorial power and natural resources and acts of hostility that expose the Bedouins of the Naqab desert to precarity and dispossession. It also tries to operate as an amplifier to the Palestinian Bedouins’ narratives and struggle for existence and recognition.
Historical Context
After the establishment of the Israeli state in 1948, 90% of the 100,000 Bedouins (Weizman, The conflict shoreline: colonization as climate change in the Negev Desert) were violently displaced out of the Naqab and by 1966 a well-tested colonial strategy came into play: seven concentration towns were built, with the intent to uproot the Bedouins from their lands and force them to abandon their pastoral and agrarian lifestyle for a hopeless urbanization that would render them an easily controllable sub-proletariat with no agency.


The Bedouins have a long history of inhabiting the Naqab desert, their existence dating back to the time of the Ottoman Empire. Despite their initial nomadic nature, by the end of the 19th century they were in the process of sedentarizing: their movement became less frequent and their settlements became permanent (Weizman, The conflict shoreline: colonization as climate change in the Negev Desert). However, after the establishment of the Israeli state in 1948, 90% of the 100,000 Bedouins were violently displaced out of the Naqab and by 1966 a well-tested colonial strategy came into play: seven concentration towns were built, with the intent to uproot the Bedouins from their lands and force them to abandon their pastoral and agrarian lifestyle for a hopeless urbanization that would render them an easily controllable sub-proletariat with no agency. That is when resistance started building up amongst the Bedouins, as many of them refused to leave their ancestral land for a precarious future, and that is when the narrative of their illegitimacy was first constructed by the Israeli state. Today, according to the Negev Coexistence Forum for Civil Equality there are 42 villages in the Naqab desert that are perceived as illegal and that suffer the consequences of Israel’s refusal to recognize them: from lack of infrastructure, educational and health services to destruction of the land and demolitions, the list of hostile acts is long. The village of Al-Araqib alone has suffered more than 100 demolitions in the past 9 years, and as recently as June 7, 2018, the entire village was demolished for the 129th time. Seeking to correct the injustice done to them, many Bedouins have resorted to filing official land claims to the Israeli court, providing documentation and evidence to substantiate their right to their ancestral lands. However, not surprisingly, the court denies the legitimacy of their claims and refuses to recognize the Bedouins as anything other than illegal nomads, while invoking the “Dead Negev doctrine” (Weizman, The conflict shoreline: colonization as climate change in the Negev Desert), which entirely erases and disregards the way Bedouins inhabit and cultivate the land.


It is important to examine how the court (and by extension the state) understands the evidence the Bedouins provide and how the denial to accept that evidence as admissible represents the state’s refusal to accept the traditions of the Bedouins as anything other than exotic and particular, in need to be modernized, controlled and Westernized. In the case of Nuri Al-Uqbi (from Al’Araqib) vs the Israeli state, a critical part of the testimonies was based on oral histories that were passed on through generations and thus constituted evidence for the Bedouin tribes: “…in a society where the oral tradition is the means of retaining the cultural memory, this memory, particularly when it is about ongoing habits and the conditions of life, is also a collective matter.” (Weizman, The conflict shoreline: colonization as climate change in the Negev Desert).. However, both the state’s lawyers and the judge dismissed most of the oral testimonies as “hearsay” unless experienced first-hand; in every other occasion “… the judge was willing to allow only the testimony of scientific experts.” It therefore seems that the claim to the right of home, territory and basic human rights for the Bedouins comes after the ability to articulate and communicate those rights on Israel’s terms (on a legislative, political and cultural basis). Israel, in its colonial project, does not just control natural, economic and military resources (more often than not, these tend to overlap) but also controls the means of knowledge and the means of production of knowledge, deploying scientific models to discredit the validity of cultural practices and with them, the Bedouins’ socio-spatial existence.


A significant effort has been made to map the localities of the unrecognized villages, tracing the absences of the Israeli map that erases them by omission. This counter-mapping endeavor is an important political tool that can be used to give agency to the Bedouins of the Naqab and even serve as evidence of existence in their official land claim cases. Nonetheless, the map must be recognized as a scientific tool that represents a Western mode of being rather than some universal truth.


The geographic map inherently ascribes importance to a place based on visibility, density and scale, thus rendering the unrecognized villages invisible in comparison to adjacent towns and cities, as seen below.

Deprivation of Resources
There are invisible systems of inclusion and exclusion that are set in place by Israel in an effort to disenfranchise the Palestinian Bedouins. The aim is to render them evident by visualizing the dependencies between the villages, the planned towns and Beersheva; the lack of educational and medical resources; the infrastructure that brings them together or stands between them. The information is drawn from the Negev Coexistence Forum for Civil Equality archive.

Acts of Hostility
The list of hostile acts is long, from uprooting trees, spraying poison on crops, plowing the land, demolishing houses, mosques and entire villages. The village of Al-Araqib alone has suffered more than 100 demolitions in the past 9 years, and as recently as June 7, 2018, the entire village was demolished for the 129th time.

The case of the unrecognized Bedouin villages in the Naqab desert has been thoroughly studied both by scholars (The Conflict Shoreline -Eyal Weizman | Indigenous (In)justice - Ahmad Amar, Ismael Abu-Saad and Oren Yiftachel | Between rights and denials: Bedouin indigeneity in the Negev/Naqab, by Oren Yiftachel as well as many others) and by human rights organizations, (the Adalah Legal Center for Arab Minority Rights in Israel | the Negev Coexistence Forum for Civil Equality) whose work has been invaluable to the struggle for recognition. This platform operates in conjunction with the existing scholarly research and relies upon it for an in-depth analysis of the Israeli- Bedouin conflict.
